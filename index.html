Introduction

The Internet has brought tremendous convenience to modern society, but at the same time, it has amplified cybersecurity risks. In South Korea, I personally observe a sense of security complacency, where many individuals underestimate the importance of cyber defense.

To raise awareness and strengthen my own skills, I began studying hacking. Although I am still in the learning stage, I believe in sharing knowledge, even in small amounts, to contribute to a broader culture of security awareness.

Definition of Hacking

Hacking refers to the act of obtaining unauthorized access to computer systems, networks, or digital information. According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), it involves circumventing security mechanisms to exploit system vulnerabilities.

Examples include:

  • Logging into an email account without permission
  • Accessing or exfiltrating confidential data
  • Executing unauthorized operations that the system should block

Types of Hackers

  • White Hat Hackers: Ethical hackers who use the same techniques as malicious hackers but only with authorization. They perform penetration testing, vulnerability assessments, and report weaknesses to improve security. (Legal)
  • Black Hat Hackers: Malicious actors who exploit systems illegally for theft, financial gain, espionage, or sabotage. Their activities include ransomware attacks, identity theft, and data breaches. (Illegal)
  • Gray Hat Hackers: Operate in the middle ground‚Äîaccessing systems without explicit permission but not with malicious intent. They often disclose vulnerabilities to the public or vendors, sometimes raising ethical and legal debates. (Ambiguous)

Why Hacking Matters in Modern Society

Cyberattacks are increasing globally. According to the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report (2023), over 80% of breaches involved stolen credentials or weak passwords. This shows that hacking is not just a technical skill, but a societal challenge that affects governments, corporations, and individuals alike.

By studying cybersecurity and ethical hacking, we can:

  • Build more secure applications and infrastructure
  • Protect sensitive data from cybercriminals
  • Contribute to a culture of security awareness and responsibility

Conclusion

Hacking itself is a neutral skill. Its value depends on intent and context. When used maliciously, it threatens society; when used ethically, it strengthens digital safety.

My goal as a learner is to continue developing cybersecurity knowledge responsibly and to contribute toward a safer and more resilient digital society.
